# Wardley Mapping

Origin: Simon Wardley. A strategic situational awareness tool that maps the landscape of capabilities and their evolution — revealing where to invest, what to outsource, and where the world is heading.

## When to Use

- Career strategy: where to invest your learning time
- Evaluating which skills will appreciate vs depreciate
- Understanding your position in a competitive landscape
- Making build-vs-buy decisions in your career development

## The Process (Adapted for Personal Strategy)

### Step 1: Identify your user need
What value do you provide? Who benefits? Start from the top.
- "My employer needs reliable, scalable data infrastructure"
- "My team needs someone who can bridge ML and systems"

### Step 2: Map the value chain
List all capabilities needed to deliver that value, from visible to invisible:

```
[User Need: Reliable data infra]
    ↓
[System Design] → [Distributed Systems] → [Cloud Platforms]
    ↓
[ML Pipeline Knowledge] → [Data Formats] → [Compute Orchestration]
    ↓
[Communication] → [Technical Writing] → [Mentoring]
```

### Step 3: Place on evolution axis
Each capability evolves through stages:

| Stage | Characteristics | Your Strategy |
|-------|----------------|---------------|
| **Genesis** | Novel, uncertain, competitive advantage | Invest heavily — this is your edge |
| **Custom** | Understood but not standardized | Build expertise, become the go-to person |
| **Product** | Well-understood, multiple solutions | Use existing tools, don't reinvent |
| **Commodity** | Ubiquitous, no competitive advantage | Outsource or automate, don't invest |

### Step 4: Identify movement
Skills evolve. What's Custom today becomes Product tomorrow.
- "Kubernetes expertise" was Genesis (2015) → Custom (2018) → Product (2022) → approaching Commodity
- "ML infrastructure" is currently Custom → early Product

### Step 5: Strategic decisions
- **Invest in** capabilities moving from Genesis → Custom (early movers win)
- **Maintain** capabilities at Custom → Product (still differentiating)
- **Stop investing in** capabilities approaching Commodity (diminishing returns)

## Example Application

**Situation:** "Where should I focus my learning at a new ML infra role?"

```
Genesis:        LLM serving optimization, AI agent infrastructure
Custom:         ML pipeline orchestration, training infra at scale
Product:        Kubernetes, Spark, general data engineering
Commodity:      SQL, basic Python, cloud console
```

**Insight:** Invest in Genesis/Custom (LLM serving, ML pipelines). Maintain Product skills. Don't spend time getting better at Commodity skills.

## Pitfalls

- **Mapping too abstractly**: Be specific about YOUR capabilities, not generic categories
- **Ignoring inertia**: Even when a skill is commoditizing, switching costs are real
- **Confusing "new to me" with "Genesis"**: A skill that's new to YOU might be Product-stage in the market

## Cross-validates with

- **Circle of Competence** — Wardley shows where to expand; Circle shows where you currently stand
- **Second-Order Thinking** — Wardley maps current landscape; second-order asks "and then what evolves next?"
