# Pareto Principle (80/20 Rule)

Origin: Vilfredo Pareto (Italian economist). Roughly 80% of effects come from 20% of causes. Applied broadly: a small number of inputs drive the majority of results.

## When to Use

- Overwhelmed with too many priorities
- Want to identify highest-leverage activities
- Reviewing how you spend time vs. results produced
- Pruning commitments, goals, or habits
- Deciding where to focus during a transition

## The Process

### Step 1: List all activities/inputs
Everything you're spending time, energy, or money on in the relevant domain.

### Step 2: Estimate the output/impact of each
What results does each activity produce? Be honest, not aspirational.

### Step 3: Rank by output-per-unit-input
Which activities produce disproportionate results?

### Step 4: Identify the vital few
Typically ~20% of activities produce ~80% of value. These are your vital few.

### Step 5: Decide what to do with the trivial many
- **Eliminate**: Stop doing it entirely
- **Delegate**: Someone else does it
- **Automate**: System does it
- **Batch**: Do it less frequently
- **Accept**: Some low-value work is unavoidable — just don't let it crowd out the vital few

## Application Areas

### Career
| Activity | Typical Impact |
|----------|---------------|
| Deep work on core projects | ~80% of reputation and impact |
| Status meetings | ~5% of value |
| Email/Slack responsiveness | ~10% of value but ~40% of time |
| Mentoring key relationships | ~20% of career advancement |

### Learning
- 20% of concepts explain 80% of the field
- Focus on the foundational 20% first, then specialize

### Goals
- Which 2-3 goals, if achieved, would render most others easier or irrelevant?
- Which goals consume time but produce little satisfaction or progress?

## The Meta-Pareto

Apply Pareto to Pareto: within the top 20%, find the top 20% again.
- 4% of activities produce ~64% of results
- This is your "one thing" — the single highest-leverage activity

## Common Patterns

| If you find... | It means... |
|----------------|-------------|
| 80% of stress from 20% of commitments | Cut those commitments |
| 80% of learning from 20% of sources | Double down on those sources |
| 80% of joy from 20% of activities | Protect those activities ruthlessly |
| 80% of progress from 20% of habits | Those habits are non-negotiable |

## Pitfalls

- **Using it to justify laziness**: Pareto isn't "do 20% of the work." It's "identify the 20% that matters most."
- **Ignoring necessary but unsexy work**: Infrastructure, health basics, relationship maintenance are high-leverage even if boring.
- **Precision trap**: It's not literally 80/20. It's a heuristic for disproportionate returns. Could be 90/10 or 70/30.
- **Static analysis**: What's high-leverage changes over time. Re-evaluate regularly.

## Cross-validates with

- **Eisenhower Matrix** — Pareto identifies high-impact; Eisenhower adds the urgency dimension
- **Theory of Constraints** — Pareto finds what matters most; TOC finds what's blocking it
