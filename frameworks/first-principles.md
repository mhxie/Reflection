# First Principles Thinking

Origin: Aristotle. "Boil things down to the most fundamental truths and reason up from there."

## When to Use

- Starting something new and conventional wisdom feels wrong
- Cost/approach seems "that's just how it's done"
- Need to innovate rather than iterate
- Challenging an assumption everyone accepts

## The Process

### Step 1: Identify assumptions
What do you currently believe about this situation? List every assumption, even obvious ones.

### Step 2: Break down to fundamentals
For each assumption, ask: "Is this a fundamental truth, or is it convention?"
- Physics, math, economics = fundamental
- "That's how we've always done it" = convention
- "Everyone does it this way" = convention

### Step 3: Reason up from fundamentals
Starting only from what's fundamentally true, what's the best approach? Ignore existing solutions entirely.

### Step 4: Reality check
Is your first-principles solution actually better, or is the convention efficient for good reason? Convention exists because it solved real problems — don't dismiss it carelessly.

## Example Application

**Situation:** "I need a CS degree to be a senior engineer"
- Assumption: degrees signal competence
- Fundamental truth: competence comes from deep understanding + practice
- First principles: what specifically does a degree provide that I can/can't get elsewhere?
- Reality check: some doors (visa, certain companies) genuinely require degrees

## Pitfalls

- **Analysis paralysis**: Don't first-principles everything. Reserve for high-stakes decisions.
- **Ignoring accumulated wisdom**: Convention often encodes lessons from failures you haven't seen.
- **Arrogance trap**: "I figured it out from scratch" when you're just poorly reinventing the wheel.

## Cross-validates with

- **Inversion** — First principles says "what's true?" Inversion says "what would make this fail?"
- **Regret Minimization** — First principles clarifies the decision; regret minimization clarifies the stakes
