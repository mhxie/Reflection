# Second-Order Thinking

Origin: Howard Marks (Oaktree Capital). "First-level thinking is simplistic and superficial. Second-level thinking is deep, complex, and convoluted."

## When to Use

- Making a decision with consequences that ripple outward
- Evaluating a "obvious" choice that seems too easy
- Policy decisions, career moves, relationship choices
- When everyone agrees on the right answer (consensus is often first-order)

## The Process

### Step 1: Identify the first-order effect
"If I do X, then Y happens." This is the obvious, immediate consequence.

### Step 2: Ask "And then what?"
For each first-order effect, trace the chain:
- "And then what happens?"
- "Who else is affected?"
- "What behaviors change?"
- "What are the second-order effects of those changes?"

### Step 3: Map the cascade
```
Action → First-order effect → Second-order effect → Third-order effect
                            → Second-order effect B
```

### Step 4: Evaluate the full picture
Sometimes the second-order effects reverse the first-order benefit.

## Example Application

**Decision:** "Take a higher-paying job with longer hours"
- 1st order: More money
- 2nd order: Less time for learning → skills plateau → fewer options later
- 2nd order: Less time with partner → relationship strain → emotional drain
- 2nd order: More money → lifestyle inflation → need the higher salary → trapped
- 3rd order: Skills plateau + trapped = exactly the opposite of career growth

**Insight:** The "obvious" choice (more money) may be the wrong one when second-order effects are traced.

## The Consensus Test

If everyone thinks X is the right answer, they're probably only doing first-order thinking. Second-order thinkers look for where the consensus is wrong.

| First-order thinking | Second-order thinking |
|---------------------|----------------------|
| "This company is great, buy the stock" | "Everyone knows it's great, so it's priced in" |
| "I should optimize for salary" | "What does optimizing for salary cost me?" |
| "I should say yes to this opportunity" | "What am I saying no to by saying yes?" |

## Pitfalls

- **Infinite regress**: You can't trace every chain forever. Stop at 2-3 orders.
- **Paralysis**: Not every decision needs second-order analysis. Reserve for consequential choices.
- **Overthinking simple things**: Sometimes the first-order answer is correct.

## Cross-validates with

- **Pre-Mortem** — Second-order traces consequences forward; Pre-mortem works backward from failure
- **Inversion** — Both look beyond the obvious, from different angles
