# Map of Meaning

Origin: Viktor Frankl (Man's Search for Meaning) + existential psychology. Meaning isn't found — it's constructed through engagement with responsibility, suffering, and creation.

## When to Use

- Feeling purposeless despite achieving goals
- Questioning whether current work matters
- Processing suffering, loss, or significant setback
- Annual or life-stage reflection
- Goals feel empty or mechanical

## Frankl's Three Sources of Meaning

### 1. Creative Values (What you give to the world)
Meaning through creating something — work, art, ideas, systems, relationships.
- What are you building that outlasts your effort?
- What would be missing from the world if you stopped?
- Where does your work create genuine value for others?

### 2. Experiential Values (What you receive from the world)
Meaning through experiencing — beauty, truth, love, nature, knowledge.
- What experiences make you feel fully alive?
- When do you lose track of time (not escapism — genuine absorption)?
- What relationships give your life depth?

### 3. Attitudinal Values (How you face what you can't change)
Meaning through choosing your stance toward unavoidable suffering.
- What difficult situation are you transforming through your response to it?
- What have you learned from hardship that you couldn't have learned otherwise?
- How has struggle shaped who you're becoming?

## The Meaning Audit

For each major life area, assess:

| Life Area | Creative (giving) | Experiential (receiving) | Attitudinal (stance) |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------------|---------------------|
| Career | What am I building? | What am I learning? | How am I handling difficulty? |
| Relationships | What am I contributing? | What richness am I receiving? | How am I growing through conflict? |
| Health | How am I caring for myself? | What does my body enable? | How do I face limitations? |
| Learning | What knowledge am I creating? | What wonder am I experiencing? | How do I handle confusion? |

## The Existential Vacuum Test

Signs that meaning is thin:
- Chasing achievements without satisfaction
- Boredom despite being busy
- Filling time with distraction
- "Is this all there is?" feeling after accomplishments
- Goals that look good on paper but feel hollow

**Antidote:** Look for where responsibility + capability + caring overlap. That's where meaning lives.

## Meaning vs Happiness

| Happiness | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| Feeling good | Feeling that it matters |
| Present-focused | Past, present, and future connected |
| Can be passive | Requires active engagement |
| Often individual | Often involves others |
| Fluctuates | More stable |
| Not always meaningful | Not always happy |

The goal isn't to choose one. It's to notice when you're optimizing for happiness at the expense of meaning, or suffering for meaning that's actually hollow.

## Pitfalls

- **Meaning martyrdom**: Suffering isn't inherently meaningful. The meaning comes from your response, not the suffering itself.
- **Meaning comparison**: Your sources of meaning are personal. Don't rank them against others'.
- **Overthinking meaning**: Sometimes meaning emerges from committed action, not philosophical analysis.
- **Nihilism shortcut**: "Nothing has inherent meaning" is technically true and practically useless. Meaning is constructed, not discovered — that makes it more powerful, not less.

## Cross-validates with

- **Ikigai** — Ikigai maps what to do; Map of Meaning asks why it matters
- **Stoic Reflection** — Both address how to face difficulty; Stoic focuses on control, Meaning focuses on purpose
