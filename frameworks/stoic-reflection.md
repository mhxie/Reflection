# Stoic Reflection

Origin: Marcus Aurelius, Seneca, Epictetus. A 2000-year-old practice of examining what you control, accepting what you don't, and building equanimity through honest self-examination.

## When to Use

- Feeling anxious or overwhelmed about things outside your control
- Processing a setback or rejection
- Need to regain perspective after emotional turbulence
- Evening reflection / journaling
- Preparing for a difficult conversation or situation

## The Core Practice: Dichotomy of Control

Divide everything into two categories:

| In Your Control | Not In Your Control |
|----------------|-------------------|
| Your effort | Outcomes |
| Your response | Others' opinions |
| Your preparation | Market conditions |
| Your attitude | The past |
| Your choices | Other people's choices |

**The discipline:** Pour energy into Column 1. Release attachment to Column 2.

## Three Stoic Reflection Exercises

### 1. Morning Premeditation (Premeditatio Malorum)
Before the day: "What could go wrong today? How would I respond?"
- Not pessimism — preparation. Rehearse equanimity.
- "If I get critical feedback today, I will listen fully before responding."
- "If this meeting goes badly, I will focus on what I can learn."

### 2. Evening Review (Seneca's Method)
Three questions at end of day:
1. **What went well?** (Acknowledge effort and good judgment)
2. **What went poorly?** (No self-flagellation — just honest diagnosis)
3. **What can I do differently?** (Actionable, specific)

### 3. View from Above (Marcus Aurelius)
Zoom out. See your situation from increasing distance:
- Your desk → your office → your city → the planet → the cosmos
- "Will this matter in 5 years? In 50?"
- Not to minimize, but to right-size

## Stoic Questions for Reflection

- "Is this in my control?" (The first question, always)
- "What would the best version of me do here?"
- "Am I reacting to reality, or to my story about reality?"
- "What am I afraid of, and is that fear based on what I control?"
- "If I lost this, what would remain?"
- "Am I pursuing this because it matters, or because I'm attached?"

## The Negative Visualization

Imagine losing something you value — not to create anxiety, but to:
1. Appreciate what you have now
2. Reduce attachment-driven anxiety
3. Prepare emotionally for possible loss
4. Clarify what truly matters vs. what you're merely habituated to

## Pitfalls

- **Emotional suppression**: Stoicism isn't about not feeling. It's about not being controlled by feelings.
- **Passivity**: "Accept what you can't control" doesn't mean "don't try." Try fully — then release attachment to the outcome.
- **Toxic positivity**: Don't use Stoic framing to dismiss real problems that need action.

## Cross-validates with

- **Pre-Mortem** — Both prepare for failure, but Stoic reflection focuses on emotional preparation while Pre-Mortem focuses on strategic preparation
- **Immunity to Change** — Stoic reflection may reveal that fear (not strategy) is the real constraint
